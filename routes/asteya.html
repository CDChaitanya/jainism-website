Asteya: The Principle of Non-Stealing

Textual Foundation

Asteya, meaning "non-stealing," is another of the fundamental virtues in Jain philosophy, emphasizing the importance of honesty and respect for what belongs to others. Derived from the Sanskrit words a (non) and steya (stealing), it prohibits not just taking what isn’t freely given but also wanting or desiring another's possessions.

The Acharanga Sutra emphasizes:

परिग्रहेणैव कर्माणि बध्यन्ते

Translation: "Attachments bind the soul to karma."

This teaching underscores how even the desire for something not ours creates karmic bonds, hindering spiritual growth. Jain texts further elaborate that Asteya applies not only to material possessions but also to time, energy, and intellectual property, urging individuals to cultivate fairness and gratitude in all aspects of life.

Personal Reflection

Learning about Asteya challenged me to reflect on my actions and mindset in ways I hadn’t considered before. While I’ve always associated stealing with tangible items, Jainism’s expansive definition of theft made me realize how everyday habits can inadvertently violate this principle.

What I Learned That I Wasn’t Aware Of

Stealing time: One idea that stood out to me was how wasting someone’s time—whether by being late, unprepared, or inattentive—can also be considered a form of theft. This insight encouraged me to be more mindful of others' time and efforts.

Mental Stealing: The concept of stealing extends to thoughts and ideas as well. Plagiarism or even taking credit for someone else’s work in group settings violates the spirit of Asteya. Reflecting on this, I realized how crucial it is to give proper acknowledgment in every aspect of life.

Contentment and Gratitude: The texts also taught me that the root of theft often lies in discontentment. By practicing gratitude for what we have, we naturally let go of the desire for more, aligning our lives with the principle of Asteya.

A New Perspective

One example that deeply affected me was a passage from a Jain text. It tells the story of a merchant known for his great wealth, but he was also famous for being dishonest in his trade. Despite his material riches, he lived a life full of fear—constantly worried about his reputation and obsessively guarding his possessions. In sharp contrast, a humble monk with no material wealth lived a peaceful life, content with whatever alms he received.

The text explained that the merchant’s fear itself was a form of Asteya. The merchant wasn’t just holding onto his material wealth but was also clinging to the fear of losing it. This fear "stole" his inner peace, making him constantly restless.

This story opened my eyes to how Asteya is not limited to the physical act of theft. It extends to the mental and emotional realm, where we may cling to things—whether material possessions, fears, or anxieties that don't truly belong to us. The monk, on the other hand, with his detachment and peace, demonstrated that true freedom comes from letting go of the need to possess, and in doing so, one can find inner peace.

What I Admired

What I admired most about Asteya is its focus on fairness and gratitude. It goes beyond just refraining from taking things and emphasizes living in harmony with others by respecting their space, time, and effort. It encourages us to cultivate a sense of inner contentment, which reduces greed and promotes generosity.

I also found it inspiring how the practice of Asteya fosters trust and community. When we live honestly and give credit where it’s due, we strengthen our relationships and contribute to a more ethical world.

Studying Asteya has motivated me to be more mindful of how I interact with others. Whether it’s respecting someone’s ideas, valuing their time, or simply being grateful for what I have, I now see these small actions as meaningful steps toward living a life rooted in non-stealing.
