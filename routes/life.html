Life of an Ascetic

The life of a Jain ascetic is defined by a deep commitment to renunciation. Jainism teaches that liberation (moksha) can only be attained by freeing oneself from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara), which is bound by the accumulation of karma. Ascetics (known as Sadhus and Sadhvis) dedicate their lives to shedding worldly attachments, desires, and material possessions in pursuit of this liberation.

Ascetics renounce not just physical possessions but also emotional and mental attachments. Their goal is to live a life of simplicity and austerity, which is considered the most effective way to cleanse the soul of accumulated karma. The ascetic's path is one of self-discipline, self-control, and detachment.

Possessions of a Jain Ascetic

Jain ascetics lead a life of profound simplicity, relying on a few essential items that hold deep spiritual and symbolic significance. Each possession aligns with the principles of Jainism, particularly ahimsa (non-violence), aparigraha (non-possessiveness), and mindfulness, emphasizing the ascetic’s detachment from material desires.

1) Rajoharana ( Broom )

The rajoharana is a small broom made of soft natural fibers, such as cotton or peacock feathers, and is used to gently sweep the ground before walking or sitting. This action ensures no harm comes to even the smallest living beings that might otherwise go unnoticed. The broom not only prevents harm but also serves as a daily practice of mindfulness, reminding the ascetic of their commitment to non-violence

2) Kamandalu/Patra (Water pot/bowl)

The patra is a simple bowl used by ascetics during their daily alms rounds. Typically made of metal, wood, or clay, it is designed to rest on a stand with four small legs, minimizing harm to microorganisms in the soil when placed on the ground. The design of the patra reflects care for life at every level, symbolizing humility, non-attachment, and the practice of depending on others while causing minimal impact.

Jain ascetics use the patra only for sustenance, reflecting their disciplined approach to food as a necessity rather than indulgence. Among Digambara monks, however, even this minimal possession is absent, as they accept food directly into their cupped hands.

3) Scriptures

Some Jain ascetics may carry sacred texts for studying and teaching. These items are not considered possessions, but tools to help with spiritual growth and sharing knowledge. They are used carefully and respectfully, highlighting the ascetic’s focus on wisdom and spiritual development.

4) Clothing

The clothing of Jain ascetics varies between the Śvētāmbara and Digambara traditions:

Śvētāmbara Clothing: Śvētāmbara ascetics wear simple, unstitched white garments, symbolizing purity and detachment. This attire is minimal and purely functional, reflecting their renunciation of material attachments.

Digambara Clothing: Digambara monks renounce clothing altogether, embracing complete nudity as a demonstration of their detachment from material possessions and their adherence to the ideal of total non-possessiveness.

A small piece of cloth called a muhapatti is often used by Śvētāmbara ascetics to cover the mouth. This prevents harm to airborne microorganisms and serves as a constant reminder of the vow of non-violence in speech.

5) Dandaka (Staff)

Some ascetics carry a dandaka, a simple staff used for balance and support during long travels. It also serves as a protective tool to gently move aside small creatures along their path. The staff represents stability and discipline, both physically and metaphorically, as it reminds them of the steady path to spiritual liberation.

Living truthfully: Jainism extends the idea of Satya to all aspects of life, including actions and thoughts. This holistic approach to truthfulness—being true not only in words but also in intentions and deeds—challenged me to reflect on the alignment between my inner values and outward behavior.

A New Perspective on Himsa

In a guest lecture, Nishi Shah shared a thought-provoking example that reshaped my understanding of Satya, or truth. She described a situation in which a monk is in a forest, and a hunter approaches him, asking if he has seen a deer pass by. The monk, instead of giving a direct answer, chooses to remain silent. Dr. Shah explained that the monk’s silence was a deliberate choice to uphold Satya in the most compassionate way. By staying silent, the monk avoids lying, yet he also prevents the potential harm that could come to the deer if its whereabouts were disclosed. This example made me realize that Satya isn't always about speaking the truth aloud. Sometimes, the most truthful and compassionate response is to withhold information that could cause harm, showing that truth must be balanced with non-violence (Ahimsa). This insight deepened my understanding of how truth can be practiced not just through words but through actions that protect and preserve life.

What I Admired

What stood out to me about Satya is that it’s not just about telling the truth but also about speaking with kindness and care. Jainism teaches that truth should never harm others, and this idea gave me a new perspective on how I communicate. It’s not enough to just be honest; the way we say things matters too

I admired how Satya encourages people to live with integrity—not just in their words but in their actions. It’s about being genuine and making sure that our thoughts, words, and deeds align. This approach doesn’t just help us stay true to ourselves but also builds trust and stronger relationships with others.

Learning about Satya has inspired me to be more thoughtful in my daily life. Whether I’m giving feedback, resolving a disagreement, or just talking with friends, I now try to make sure that my honesty is paired with compassion. This balance between truth and care has made my interactions more meaningful and positive.
