Life of an ascetic

Life of an Ascetic

The life of a Jain ascetic is defined by a deep commitment to renunciation. Jainism teaches that liberation (moksha) can only be attained by freeing oneself from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara), which is bound by the accumulation of karma. Ascetics (known as Sadhus and Sadhvis) dedicate their lives to shedding worldly attachments, desires, and material possessions in pursuit of this liberation.

Ascetics renounce not just physical possessions but also emotional and mental attachments. Their goal is to live a life of simplicity and austerity, which is considered the most effective way to cleanse the soul of accumulated karma. The ascetic's path is one of self-discipline, self-control, and detachment.

Possessions of a Jain Ascetic

Jain ascetics lead a life of profound simplicity, relying on a few essential items that hold deep spiritual and symbolic significance. Each possession aligns with the principles of Jainism, particularly ahimsa (non-violence), aparigraha (non-possessiveness), and mindfulness, emphasizing the ascetic’s detachment from material desires.

1) Rajoharana (Broom)

The rajoharana is a small broom made of soft natural fibers, such as cotton or peacock feathers, and is used to gently sweep the ground before walking or sitting. This action ensures no harm comes to even the smallest living beings that might otherwise go unnoticed. The broom not only prevents harm but also serves as a daily practice of mindfulness, reminding the ascetic of their commitment to non-violence.

2) Kamandalu/Patra (Water pot/bowl)

The patra is a simple bowl used by ascetics during their daily alms rounds. Typically made of metal, wood, or clay, it is designed to rest on a stand with four small legs, minimizing harm to microorganisms in the soil when placed on the ground. The design of the patra reflects care for life at every level, symbolizing humility, non-attachment, and the practice of depending on others while causing minimal impact. Jain ascetics use the patra only for sustenance, reflecting their disciplined approach to food as a necessity rather than indulgence. Among Digambara monks, however, even this minimal possession is absent, as they accept food directly into their cupped hands.

3) Scriptures

Some Jain ascetics may carry sacred texts for studying and teaching. These items are not considered possessions, but tools to help with spiritual growth and sharing knowledge. They are used carefully and respectfully, highlighting the ascetic’s focus on wisdom and spiritual development.

4) Clothing

Śvētāmbara Clothing: Śvētāmbara ascetics wear simple white garments, symbolizing purity and detachment. This attire is minimal and purely functional, reflecting their renunciation of material attachments.

Digambara Clothing: Digambara monks renounce clothing altogether, embracing complete nudity as a demonstration of their detachment from material possessions and their adherence to the idea of total non-possessiveness. However, female nuns in the Digambara sect wear a simple white saree, symbolizing purity, simplicity and their commitment to spiritual discipline.

A small piece of cloth called a muhapatti is often used by ascetics to cover the mouth. This prevents harm to airborne microorganisms, reflecting the vow of non-violence by avoiding the unintentional inhalation of even the smallest living beings.

5) Dandaka (Stick)

Some ascetics carry a dandaka, a simple stick used for balance and support during long travels.

Daily Routine and Spiritual Practices

Jain ascetics follow a strict routine that helps them maintain spiritual focus and discipline. Their life is highly regulated, with every moment spent in the pursuit of self-purification and non-attachment.

Meditation and Prayers: The day begins before dawn with meditation (dhyana) and prayers. The early morning is considered the best time for spiritual activities due to its peaceful nature. The ascetics meditate, focusing their thoughts on achieving liberation. Prayers to the Tirthankaras (spiritual teachers) and recitations of mantras are also a part of the morning rituals.

Fasting: Fasting is a way for Jain ascetics to practice self-discipline and renunciation. It helps them purify their body and mind, reduce karma, and strengthen their focus on spiritual goals. Different levels of fasting are practiced, from eating very little to completely abstaining from food and water for several days. During fasting, ascetics dedicate their time to meditation and spiritual reflection, aiming to detach from physical needs and worldly distractions. Through fasting, Jain ascetics strengthen their commitment to the path of renunciation and advance toward spiritual growth.

Dietary Restrictions: Jain ascetics follow a strict vegetarian diet, avoiding any food that involves the harm or killing of living beings. The food they eat is simple, often consisting of fruits, vegetables, and grains that have been prepared with utmost care to avoid harming any life. For instance:

  • 1. They do not consume root vegetables (like onions, garlic, potatoes) because harvesting these often kills the plant.
  • 2. The food must be prepared without harming any life, and only food that has been offered to them through the practice of alms (bhiksha) is accepted.
  • 3. They avoid fermented foods, as fermentation is believed to contain small life forms.

Bhiksha: One of the most significant aspects of a Jain ascetic’s life is the practice of begging for food. They do not cook for themselves or accept food from a family member or close acquaintance, as doing so would create attachments. Instead, they go from house to house, asking for food, which symbolizes their detachment from worldly comforts. They avoid eating food prepared just for them, staying true to the principle of non-violence. They do not request specific foods, ensuring their acceptance is impartial and non-selective. The food they receive is taken only if it is offered with respect and out of generosity. The act of begging fosters humility and reinforces their renunciation of material wealth and desires.

Silence (Mauna): Many Jain ascetics practice silence for a significant part of their day. Silence, known as mauna, helps them control their speech, which is considered a powerful tool that can either harm or help others. Ascetics practice silence to avoid causing harm with their words and to maintain a state of inner peace and self-reflection. This practice is also essential for focusing their mind on spiritual growth and meditation.

Living Arrangements

Nomadic Lifestyle: A Jain ascetic's life is typically nomadic, meaning they do not stay in one place for more than one night. They travel from town to town, village to village, seeking alms and engaging in spiritual practices. This lifestyle reinforces the principle of detachment, as ascetics do not form lasting attachments to any place, person, or community.

Caturmas (Rainy Season Retreat): During the rainy season, known as Caturmas, Jain ascetics follow a different practice. The Caturmas lasts for about four months (typically July to October), during which ascetics refrain from traveling. The reason for this is to prevent the inadvertent harm to small insects or creatures that might be killed while walking or traveling through the fields during the rains. During Caturmas, ascetics settle in one place and remain there for the entire period.
While staying in one location during Caturmas, ascetics engage in more intensive meditation, prayer, and study of the scriptures. This period of staying in one place symbolizes a temporary retreat for reflection and self-purification.

Behavior and Conduct

Jain ascetics follow the five great vows, which are the foundation of their moral conduct:

  • Ahimsa (Non-violence): The ascetics take great care to avoid harming any living being, whether it be through thought, speech, or action.
  • Satya (Truthfulness): They speak only the truth and avoid harmful gossip or lying.
  • Asteya (Non-stealing): Jain ascetics do not take anything that is not freely given to them. This includes not accepting any food or possessions unless offered in the spirit of charity.
  • Brahmacharya (Celibacy): Ascetics practice celibacy, avoiding any form of sexual activity. This vow helps them conserve their energy for spiritual practice.
  • Aparigraha (Non-possessiveness): Ascetics own as little as possible, living with extreme simplicity and detachment from material possessions.

Tapas: The Mortification of the Body in Jainism

Tapas in Jainism, derived from the Sanskrit root TAP (meaning "to heat" or "to suffer"), is a spiritual and physical discipline aimed at purifying the soul, eliminating karma, and achieving moksha (liberation). It is not merely self-mortification but a transformative process, viewed as burning away impurities through intense spiritual and physical practices.

Forms of Tapas

The life of a Jain ascetic often involves severe physical austerities that test the limits of endurance and demonstrate unparalleled commitment to the spiritual path. Here are some notable forms of Tapas, as shared by the instructor:

Jal Tapas

In this form of austerity, the ascetic meditates under a leaking pot of cold water, often on extremely cold days. This practice signifies resilience and a willingness to endure discomfort while maintaining inner calm and focus.

Dhuni Tapas

Here, the ascetic meditates amidst fire or under the blazing sun, often on scorching hot days. This practice requires exceptional endurance and signifies the burning away of karmic impurities through suffering.

Urdhvabahu

The ascetic holds one or both arms aloft for extended periods, sometimes for years, as an act of devotion and penance. The body undergoes significant transformation, including the withering of unused limbs, which becomes a testament to the ascetic's resolve.

Kareshvari

In this form, the ascetic meditates while standing on one leg, often for long durations. This posture induces physical strain and pain, testing the ascetic's ability to remain focused and detached.
